The Kākāpō — A Parrot Unlike Any Other

New Zealand’s moonlit, flightless, remarkably heavy parrot — and the patient work of bringing it back from the edge of extinction

July 13, 2026

Filed Under

Curious Creatures

Rare and unusual animals shaped by adaptation, instinct, survival, and quiet intelligence.

On a still night on Whenua Hou, a small forested island in the far south of Aotearoa New Zealand, the bush makes a sound most people will never hear anywhere else on Earth. It starts low, almost below hearing: a deep, resonant thud that seems to rise from the ground itself. Boom. A pause. Boom again. The sound repeats through the night, carrying a message across dark forest.

It comes from a male kākāpō standing in a shallow bowl he has dug into a hillside, inflating an air sac and calling patiently for a mate. The kākāpō, or Strigops habroptilus, is the world’s only flightless parrot, the heaviest parrot on Earth, nocturnal, herbivorous and among the longest-lived of birds. Males can weigh up to four kilograms, and the species is thought to live for roughly sixty to ninety years. [1][2]

A Parrot Unlike Any Other

Its Māori name means “night parrot”: kākā for parrot, pō for night. European observers called it the owl parrot, and the comparison still fits. The kākāpō has a pale, owl-like face, a large grey bill and moss-green plumage mottled with yellow and black. On a forest floor it can look less like a bird than a piece of living lichen. [1][2]

Everything about its body tells the story of a bird shaped by isolation. Its short rounded wings are no longer capable of true flight, yet they help it balance when climbing and soften its descent when it drops from a tree. Its strength lies elsewhere: powerful feet, excellent climbing ability, and camouflage so effective that it once protected the bird from predators hunting from the air.

The kākāpō did not become flightless because it was poorly adapted. It became flightless because, for a very long time, the ground was safe.

A Mating Call That Travels Through the Dark

Kākāpō do not breed every year. Their breeding is linked to mast seasons, when rimu trees produce fruit in extraordinary abundance. These events arrive irregularly, often every two to four years, and the forest must offer enough food for females to raise chicks. In a breeding season, a female lays a small clutch, but she usually fledges only one chick. [3][4]

When a mast arrives, males climb to ridges and hilltops and excavate shallow display bowls. These sites form a lek: a communal courtship ground where males call but do not form pairs or defend a nest. The kākāpō is the world’s only lek-breeding parrot. For hours after dark, a male booms from his bowl, then adds a sharp “ching” that helps a female locate him. [1][2]

A female may travel kilometres to a calling male. After mating, his role is over. She alone incubates the eggs for about a month and raises the chicks, leaving the nest to forage at night. That single-parent system is one reason the species became so vulnerable once mammalian predators arrived. [2][6]

The Cost of Being Defenceless

Before human arrival, kākāpō were widespread in New Zealand’s forests. Their survival strategy made sense in a land without ground-dwelling mammalian predators: stay still, blend in, and let danger pass overhead. The arrival of people changed that equation. Dogs, rats, cats, stoats and ferrets hunt by scent as well as sight, and the kākāpō’s habit of freezing on the forest floor became a trap rather than a defence. [1][2]

The decline was brutal. By the late twentieth century, the species had disappeared from the mainland, and feral cats were devastating the last known population on Rakiura/Stewart Island. Between 1980 and 1997, surviving birds were moved to offshore sanctuaries. By 1995, only fifty-one kākāpō remained. [5]

The Rescue: Individual by Individual

The recovery programme did not rescue the kākāpō with a single dramatic intervention. It has been a long accumulation of careful decisions: removing predators from refuge islands, fitting birds with transmitters, monitoring nests, protecting chicks, managing supplementary food and tracking the family history of a population that is still genetically fragile. Today, breeding populations are managed on Whenua Hou/Codfish Island, Pukenui/Anchor Island and Te Kākahu/Chalky Island. [3][5]

The science has become more precise without becoming simple. Researchers use DNA data to guide genetic management and artificial insemination, seeking to preserve rare lineages and reduce the cost of inbreeding. The recovery team’s own research records high levels of infertility and early embryo loss; a peer-reviewed study also found that early embryo mortality, not infertility alone, explains much of the species’ poor hatching success. [7][8]

That detail matters. It prevents an easy conservation story: a rising headcount is encouraging, but it does not erase the biological consequences of the population bottleneck. Recovery is not merely about producing more chicks. It is about building a population capable of staying healthy through generations. [7][8]

Sirocco: The Bird Who Made the World Look

For a species that lives beyond public reach, the kākāpō has an unexpectedly famous ambassador. Sirocco, a male hand-reared after a respiratory illness as a chick, imprinted strongly on people and proved unlikely to breed. That made him unusual, but it also made him visible. [10]

In 2009, while zoologist Mark Carwardine was filming the BBC series Last Chance to See with Stephen Fry, Sirocco climbed onto Carwardine’s head and attempted to court him. The footage became a worldwide sensation. In 2010, New Zealand’s Prime Minister named Sirocco the country’s Official Spokesbird for Conservation. The moment was funny; the outcome was serious: a bird most people had never heard of suddenly had a public face. [10]

Where the Kākāpō Live Now

The kākāpō is a taonga species to Ngāi Tahu, a treasured bird bound to cultural identity as well as biodiversity. Most birds are protected on predator-free southern islands, where access is tightly controlled. In 2023, four males were moved to Sanctuary Mountain Maungatautari, a fenced mainland sanctuary, to test whether kākāpō could live safely beyond their established offshore sites. It was a habitat trial, not a breeding release, and the distinction matters. [1][9]

The long-term challenge is space. The breeding islands cannot hold unlimited numbers, and crowding adds risk from competition, disease, pests and natural disasters. The recovery programme’s larger ambition is not to keep every kākāpō under lifelong individual management. It is to create larger, safer populations that can eventually thrive with far less intervention. Predator-free Rakiura/Stewart Island is one of the places being explored in that future. [11]

The 2026 Breeding Season: Hope, Without a Shortcut

On 6 January 2026, DOC announced the first kākāpō breeding season since 2022. The population then stood at 236 birds, including 83 breeding-age females. The team described the season as potentially its biggest in three decades, but it also made clear that success could not be reduced to a headline chick count. [4]

That caution is not pessimism. It is honesty. Public records later listed 235 kākāpō alive, while the season continued and chicks hatched. A live nest stream followed a female named Rakiura on Whenua Hou; by May, chicks from the monitored nest had reached the fledging stage or were close to it. It is a powerful sign of what close monitoring can protect, but not a final verdict on the species’ future. [1][6]

Why a Flightless Parrot Still Matters

It is tempting to reduce the kākāpō to its oddities: the fat parrot, the booming call, the bird that once tried to court a cameraman. But its real significance is larger. It is a surviving fragment of an older New Zealand, a forest world in which a ground-dwelling parrot could grow slow, heavy and trusting because nothing on the forest floor had evolved to hunt it.

That world is gone. What remains is a demanding question: can people repair enough of it for the kākāpō to belong there again? The answer will not be delivered by a single fruitful season, a viral clip or a conservation slogan. It will be built island by island, habitat by habitat, generation by generation.

For now, on a few protected islands in the far south, green, moon-faced parrots continue to climb trees, freeze at the crack of a twig, and boom into the dark. Somewhere beyond the ridge, a female may still be listening.
